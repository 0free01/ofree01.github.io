The main features of an ocean ecosystem include the flow of nutrients through food chains, in which plankton is eaten by fish, which are then eaten by larger predators such as sharks or whales. Currents help move warm and cold water around, as well as nutrients and microorganisms. Deep, shallow, warm and cold areas of water support different types of organisms, all having different adaptations that help them thrive in their habitats. In the ocean, there are different parts to a functioning ecosystem; the producers, the consumers and the decomposers. The producers make their own food using the sun and nutrients they find around deep-sea vents. Phytoplankton, seaweed, kelp, seagrasses and some bacteria are all producers. The consumers, who eat (consume) other animals, include Zooplankton (eat phytoplankton), Fish, Squid, Jellyfish, Crabs, Starfish and larger predators like sharks, seals, dolphins and whales. Finally, there are the Decomposers who break down material and recycle nutrients; they include marine bacteria, fungi, some worms and small bottom-dwelling organisms. These animals all interact with each other in a few main ways, including symbiosis, competition and predator-prey relationships. Fish eat zooplankton, and sharks eat fish. This controls the population of certain species and keeps ecosystems balanced. Symbiosis can be seen in coral, where bacteria give food to the coral, and the coral gives the bacteria a place to live. Finally, organisms compete for things like food, space and light, like corals and algae competing for sunlight and warm places in tropical places. </p>

echo createSection("Part B: Research on the Algal Bloom", "
<div class='intro-card'>
    <h3>What is an algal bloom and how does it form?</h3>
        <p>An algal bloom is a rapid increase of algae in water, commonly caused by an excess of nutrients, like nitrogen and phosphorus from fertilisers or runoff from farms, cities and other ecosystems. Warm temperatures, sunlight, and still water also contribute to the formation of algal blooms. Some blooms are harmless, however other ones, called harmful algal blooms (HABs), can make toxins that damage ecosystems and the quality of the water. Some different types of algal blooms are, Phytoplankton blooms (makes the water green, base of food chain, sometimes to many; making it a bloom), Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae, which is toxic and forms surface scums), Dinoflagellates (cause 'red tides,' which can poison marine life) and finally Diatoms, which are usually harmless, creating oxygen in the water. </p>
    <h3>Algal bloom along the Fleurieu Peninsula</h3>
        <p>The algal bloom in South Australia, on the Fleurieu Peninsula, is one of the largest and longest lasting blooms in Australia's history. It started in mid-March of 2025 and is caused by a harmful microalga (Karenia). At the moment, scientists believe that the algal bloom started because of a combination of natural and human factors, including a marine heatwave (causing warmer ocean temperatures), pollution of nutrients from when the Murray River flooded, and upwelling currents, bringing nutrients and dormant Algie from the depths of the ocean to the surface - en.wikipedia.org. Climate change is also considered to be a major contributing factor, because it increases water temperatures, which in turn supports algal growth.  </p>
    <h3>Impacts</h3>
        <p>Short term, algal blooms can kill marine life by releasing toxins and reducing oxygen levels in the water. Long-term impacts include damage to ecosystems, loss of biodiversity, and economic effects on fishing and tourism industries. People can also be affected by the algal bloom. Some people experience coughing, irritation of the eyes, and skin irritation when exposed to contaminated water or sea spray.  </p>
        <p>A wide range of marine organisms are affected. Producers like phytoplankton are involved in the bloom itself, but consumers such as fish, shellfish, seabirds, and even larger animals like sharks and marine mammals are affected. Research suggest that hundreds of species have been impacted, including endangered species like leafy sea dragons. Many of these creatures are affected because their food sources or oxygen levels are depleted, if one animal higher up the food chain loses population, then the animals further down are affected as well. </p>
    <h3>Signs of the Algal Bloom</h3>
        <p>There are signs of the algal bloom in Victor Harbor and Middleton, including discoloured water, foam (both on the beaches and out in the water), bad smells and dead marine life washed up on beaches. There is also a lot more seaweed that then decomposes on beaches, contributing to the smell. In some areas, the water appears murky or mixed with bubbles and foam.  </p>
    <h3>Management stratagies</h3>
        <p>Management strategies can be seen across the impacted areas. Government agencies like PIRSA and SA Health have sent out warnings that advise people to avoid discoloured or foamy water. Monitoring systems, like water testing and satellite tracking, are being used to track the bloom. In some cases, fishing restrictions and shellfish farm closures have been put in place to protect public health and allow ecosystems to recover. </p>
    <h3>Perspectives</h3>
        <p>The perspectives of the local community's show that people are concerned and frustrated about how the environment is coping with the bloom. Many local people and businesses are worried about the damage to the environment, tourism and the long-term impacts on their way of life and communities. Overall, the bloom has taught communities that ocean ecosystems can be vulnerable and that it Is important to manage the impacts that our actions are making on climate change. </p>
